Assignment 4 - Becoming an Independent Data Scientist¶

Project - Irreligion Ratio vs. SHI & GRI¶

Author: Shanlang Liu
Date: 2019 Aug 7

Domain: Religion, different regions worldwide

Question: What is the irreligion ratio of each region, and how much is it affected by social and regulatory factors?

Datasets:
All the datasets incorporated are retrieved from Pew Research Center

  • Social Hostilities Index Score in 2016
  • Worldwide All Population, 2010
  • Worldwide All Population, 2020
  • Worldwide Unaffiliated Population, 2010
  • Worldwide Unaffiliated Population, 2020

For the audience with interest to examine or validate the queston in greater depth, the index are elaborated in the links.

  • Social Hostilities Index Score (SHI)
  • Government Restrictions Index Score (GRI)

Project Briefing:

To complete the visualization, a dataset is expected to include 2016 Irreligion Ratio grouped by region and Social Hostilities Index (SHI), Government Restrictions Index (GRI) in accordance.

Unfortunately, there seems not to be any available data for 2016 religion population, so 2010 data and 2020 estimation are imported to linearly extrapolate 2016 irreligion and all population, and then calculate the ratio with a simple division.

2016 SHI and GRI comes together in another dataset, and everthing needed is there once this dataset is merged with the forementioned one.

The first bit of the analysis is, unsuprisingly, to examine how SHI and GRI are correlated with irreligion ratio. As per the given data, the correlations are about -0.205 and -0.212 respectively, neither of which is significant. A scatterplot is placed to demonstrate it visually.

The final output is a bubble chart which is more of a descriptive than an analytical visualization, and three variable are there in the chart. Irreligion ratio is reflected by the bubble size while SHI and GRI are marked on the two axes. Top fifteen regions are highlighted, five of which are labeled with region name.

The observation is less intuitive as expected. Most of the highly irreligious regions are with low SHI and GRI, which contradicts a common sense. Combined with the low correlations examined, such distribution implies that social and regulatory factors are not as influential as one may expect. Further research might need to be done if more data dimensions are available to investigate more correlated factors.

''' To clean the data with the following steps:

    1. Remove continent level regions with NaN value while keep Namibia;
    2. Remove all observations with < 10000 unaffiliated population as these data would be less accurate
    3. Drop all ['ISO_code'] columns as there is no further use;
    4. Trim space and double quotes throughout the entire dataframe;
    5. Convert population data from string to integer '''

nan_rows = df['Region'][df['ISO code'].isnull()].tolist()
nan_rows.remove(' "Namibia"')
df = (df[(~df['Region'].isin(nan_rows)) & 
         (df['Unaffiliated 2010'] != ' "<10000"') &
         (df['Unaffiliated 2020'] != ' "<10000"')]
      .drop('ISO code', axis = 1)
      .apply(lambda x: x.str.strip(' ').str.strip('"'))
)
df[['Unaffiliated 2010', 'All 2010', 'Unaffiliated 2020', 'All 2020']] = (
 df[['Unaffiliated 2010', 'All 2010', 'Unaffiliated 2020', 'All 2020']].astype(int)
)

''' To estimate 2016 unaffliliated ratio with the following two steps:
    
    1. To estimate 2016 unaffliliated and whole population using the sum of 2010 and 2020 data multiplied with 0.6
    2. To work out 2016 unaffiliated ratio, round to 3 decimal places '''

df['Unaffiliated 2016'] = 0.6 * (df['Unaffiliated 2010'] + df['Unaffiliated 2020'])
df['All 2016'] = 0.6 * (df['All 2010'] + df['All 2020'])
df['Unaffiliated Ratio 2016'] = (df['Unaffiliated 2016'] / df['All 2016']).round(3)
